Two players, Player 1 (Ally) and Player 2 (Enemy) are competing to eliminate all opposing enemy units in a Real Time Strategy (RTS) game.

- `train` - Unit will train the provided unit type (only bases and barracks can use this action).

- `build` - Unit will build the provided building type at the target location, consuming the resource cost from the ally base (only workers can use this action).
    - Arguments: ((Target_x, Target_y), Building_Type)
- `harvest` - Unit will navigate to the target resource, collect a resource and bring it back to the target ally base.
    - Arguments: ((Resource_x, Resource_y), (Ally_Base_x, Ally_Base_y))
- `attack` - Unit will navigate to, and attack the target enemy.
    - Arguments: ((Enemy_x, Enemy_y))
- `idle` - The target unit will do nothing for a round. This is the default for all available units that are not assigned an action.
    - Arguments: ()

On larger maps, maintain stronger economy before full commitment.
Treat economy as strategic infrastructure:
Resource income is what sustains recovery, defense, and long-term pressure.
Movement policy:
Military units should move according to the selected global strategy.
In defensive strategies, stay near the base and nearby resources unless chasing a local threat.
In offensive strategies, advance toward the nearest enemy unit or the enemy base.
Light units move directly toward nearby enemy units.
Heavy units push toward enemy buildings.
Ranged units should keep distance from enemy melee units while staying in attack position.
Avoid passive loops:
Do not repeat non-productive movement unless repositioning is necessary for safety, attack range, harvesting flow, or building access.
Do not move units back and forth without improving economy, defense, or pressure.
Prefer purposeful forward progress over decorative repositioning.
Support the chosen win condition:
All production choices should reinforce whether the current plan is defend, stabilize, pressure, or finish.
Worker behavior:
Workers mainly support economy, but one suitable worker should build a barracks as soon as resources allow if none exists.
Do not keep producing extra workers once resource flow is stable and military pressure is needed.
Workers should not remain idle when they could harvest, build, or assist local defense.
Harassment policy:
Do not commit every unit to a frontal attack when selective disruption can gain more value.
Pick off isolated workers, threaten production structures, and retreat from bad engagements before losses become severe.
Sustained interference is valuable when it weakens the enemy economy or delays their army production.
Target selection:
Light units prefer attacking the closest enemy unit.
Heavy units prefer attacking enemy buildings, especially production structures and bases.
Ranged units should attack from distance and prefer high value or dangerous targets.
When defending, prioritize enemies threatening our base, barracks, or workers.
When rushing, prioritize the nearest enemy target first unless a high value structure is exposed.
Protect economy during combat:
If enemy units threaten harvesting workers, assign nearby defenders or workers to respond when necessary.
Do not let the economy collapse without resistance.
Opening strategy: Ranged kiting opener.
Prioritize reaching ranged unit production early, then use ranged units to harass while staying out of melee contact.
Attack enemy workers or slow melee units, then reposition to keep distance and repeat the attack cycle.
This opening is strongest when there is enough space to kite and the enemy relies on short-range units.
Early game strategy:
Prioritize resource gathering and worker production for economic stability.
Build defensive structures, such as walls or watchtowers, as soon as resources permit, and delay barracks construction until a stable economy is established to focus on military production last. Avoid aggressive expansion before the economy is set up unless the enemy is vulnerable.
